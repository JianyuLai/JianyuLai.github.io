Respiratory viruses spread through the air, but the indoor environment is not a passive backdrop — ventilation, air exchange rates, and building infrastructure actively shape how far and how fast airborne pathogens travel. This work examines how ventilation modifies infection risk in real-world shared settings.

## Ventilation in College Residence Halls

A study following college students in residence hall cohorts examined how building-level ventilation rates related to respiratory virus transmission across multiple seasons. Using a causal inference framework, the analysis estimated the effect of ventilation on infection risk while accounting for other behavioral and environmental factors.

The findings suggest that higher air exchange rates are associated with reduced transmission of influenza and other respiratory viruses — consistent with the hypothesis that airborne spread is a dominant route in densely occupied indoor spaces. The study also demonstrates that observational cohort designs, when carefully analyzed, can yield causal-quality inference about environmental determinants of transmission.

## Why It Matters

Ventilation is one of the few modifiable environmental factors available at the building and population scale. Unlike individual-level interventions (masks, antivirals), infrastructure-based air quality improvements can reduce transmission without requiring ongoing behavioral compliance. Understanding the quantitative relationship between air exchange and infection risk is essential for evidence-based building and public health policy.
