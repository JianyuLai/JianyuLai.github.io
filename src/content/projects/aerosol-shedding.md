How much infectious virus does an infected person exhale — and what determines that amount? This research line uses the Gesundheit-II exhaled breath aerosol (EBA) sampler to measure viral output directly from the breath of SARS-CoV-2-infected individuals, examining how variant, vaccination status, and mask type each shape the aerosol dose entering shared air.

## Variant Shedding

Among 93 individuals with COVID-19, those infected with Alpha, Delta, or Omicron shed significantly more viral RNA into exhaled breath aerosols than individuals infected with ancestral lineages or variants not associated with increased transmissibility. Each of the three highly transmissible variants had evolved this elevated-shedding phenotype independently — a convergent outcome consistent with natural selection favoring strains with greater capacity for airborne spread.

## Mask Efficacy as Source Control

A companion study applied the same Gesundheit-II platform to compare exhaled viral aerosol output across mask conditions. N95 respirators substantially reduced the viral RNA content of exhaled breath compared to surgical masks, which offered more modest source control. The findings provide direct experimental evidence that the type of mask — not simply whether a mask is worn — matters for interrupting transmission at the source.

## Influenza Shedding by Infection Route and Severity

A third study extended the Gesundheit-II platform to influenza A(H3), comparing exhaled aerosol shedding between individuals with community-acquired infection and those nasally inoculated under experimental conditions, and examining how symptom severity — mild versus moderate — shapes aerosol output. The comparison addresses a question central to interpreting transmission studies: whether shedding observed in challenge models reflects what happens in naturally acquired infection.

## Why It Matters

Exposure risk is not simply a function of proximity to an infected person. Shedding varies by orders of magnitude across individuals and is further shaped by which variant they carry and what they are wearing. Together, these studies show that airborne transmission risk is driven by measurable, modifiable factors — and that high-filtration respirators worn by infectious individuals can meaningfully reduce the viral aerosol entering shared spaces.
